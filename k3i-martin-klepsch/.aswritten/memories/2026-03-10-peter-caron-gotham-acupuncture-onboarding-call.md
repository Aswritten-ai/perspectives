# Onboarding Call — Peter Caron, Gotham Acupuncture

Demo and discovery call with Peter Caron of Gotham Acupuncture. Scarlet demoed aswritten.ai's collective memory system, then transitioned into an extended interview about Peter's business model, expansion plans, and go-to-market strategy.

## Business Overview

Peter Caron and Jordan Barber are combining their solo acupuncture practices on the Upper West Side of Manhattan into a high-end managed care practice under the Gotham Acupuncture brand.

Peter describes the partnership:

> "Both Jordan and I have solo practices on the Upper West Side. We work well together and Jordan has multiple practices that run without his influence already on Long Island. They are all insurance based and they're all mid-range practices in terms of cloud and fanciness. And so what we are doing is we are combining our resources to start a very high end practice on the Upper West Side."

On how they met:

> "I knew his reputation. Both of us have had similar jobs within the field. Both of us taught at Pacific College at different times, I was vice president of ASNEY. He the state association, He was the president, like eight years before that. It's like we knew each other abstractly."

Jordan's background: pelvic floor specialist, background somewhat similar to Peter's Guatemala experience. He started at the current space last summer.

They are also co-authoring a book together, completed in a 4-month sprint. Still wrapping up the conclusion and Peter's author intro.

## The Managed Care Model — Selling Outcomes, Not Services

The core business model shift is from traditional single-session acupuncture to a managed care model built around courses of treatment with defined outcomes.

Peter on the model:

> "We are changing our business model to not be about you come to me when you want to but instead courses of treatment so that we are selling outcomes as opposed to services. That's one of the huge differences."

How it works in practice:

> "This will be a managed care model which basically means that I see a new patient and then I put forward a treatment plan for something like the next six weeks and they see assistants to implement that treatment plan and then they come back to me. So that instead of income exclusively coming from me seeing patients, and instead of patients being frankly over-served with expensive attention from me, when sometimes we just have to do the next step in the process, they're able to see someone else for that."

> "This involves me seeing patients for a full hour and doing a long breakdown. And then seeing assistants for... people an hour. So they get deep detailed analysis from myself or from Jordan, depending on their case and then they go through a course of treatment. And then they return to us. We'll be high touch with the assistants. We'll be looking in. We might even be poking our heads into the treatment rooms while this is going on and kind of checking in, getting a little bit of like a white glove service to this."

> "And this should allow us to greatly increase our revenue and provide a pretty comparable service."

## Four Clinical Niches

Peter is shifting from general practice to focused niches for better branding and streamlined care:

> "One of the things that we need to do to make this work is that I'm switching largely from being a general practitioner to focusing on a few niches. This should help with marketing and branding, in addition to allowing us to streamline these processes."

The four niches across Peter and Jordan:

1. **Post-stroke recovery** (Peter's side)
2. **Pelvic floor care** (Jordan — already a specialist)
3. **Digestion** (Peter's side)
4. **Anxiety/depression** (Peter's side — "mental health")

Peter on why niching matters:

> "We certainly run into the issue with marketing that if you're like, I do everything, then you're kind of an expert in nothing as far as at least public perception, even if you happen to be an expert in everything, it's just bad branding."

## B2B Referral Marketing Model

The marketing strategy is primarily B2B — building referral relationships with other practitioners rather than direct-to-consumer.

Peter on current referral sources:

> "Basically all of my referrals at this point come from other practitioners. Many of them come from MDs... I'd say that makes up about 60 to 70% of my referrals. The rest are pretty much... either PTs or concierge doctors."

On the Upper West Side market:

> "In a different market this might not work as well, but on the Upper West Side, we have infinite practitioners. We have functionally infinite practitioners, and functionally infinite patients."

Scarlet added the key insight: "And functionally infinite budgets amongst patients to tolerate the best service available."

Peter confirmed: "Exactly."

## The Pelvic Floor PT Referral Pitch

Peter has developed a specific B2B pitch for pelvic floor physical therapists. This emerged from networking with Jordan's PT connections.

> "If you have a patient who's coming in as too much trauma or too much central sensitization or other types of nervous system dysfunction, they're going to be unable to complete your course of treatment for the pelvic floor because you're going to be regulated. You're gonna be attempting to regulate their nervous system the whole time."

The pitch to PTs:

> "If you have this type of patient, they are likely to drop out of care. Which means that they're unhappy with the results. And it also means you don't get paid. So that's losing across the board for you and for your patient. You send them to me, we do three to five sessions, we regulate their nervous system. I bounce them back to you with a report on their case, and then we either continue working together to help the patient or they're basically done with me and you take over from there. This allows the patient to actually complete the course of treatment that they need so that they don't continue to have this issue and they don't drop out, which means that you get paid better, your patients get better results and their patients are happier."

Scarlet validated this from personal experience:

> "I had exactly this happen to me about a month ago, went to a pelvic floor PT and got an internal exam and it was way too much for me. And I could not go back and I still have a pelvic floor problem."

## Post-Stroke Recovery Opportunity

Peter identified post-stroke care as a strategic niche:

> "One of the reasons we're considering post-stroke care as one of our services is because acupuncture is good at it. Western medicine is bad at it and because protocols that are well validated in the scientific literature involved three sessions a week."

> "It's good money, it's good business, it's an empty market, it's easy to pitch to doctors. It's like across the board that's a pretty good set of things."

Peter noted they may adapt the protocols:

> "Realistically, I think we'll only be partially following these sorts of technical recommendations from the scientific literature because I don't necessarily think they're offering up the best approach, but this creates buy-in for us to do three sessions a week with a post-stroke."

## Expansion — Space Decision

Currently deciding between two paths for physical expansion:

> "We're either taking over the whole space or move into a new space and because they're just moving slow and deciding whether they want to give it up or not. We're like, look, we're moving forward with other spaces, you have until we signed a lease to make a decision."

## Team-Based Care Philosophy

Peter's evolving insight about working in practitioner teams:

> "More and more I'm finding some of the best results I get are when I'm part of a team of practitioners, which also means that I can't be trying to do everything the team does."

His existing endpoints were already getting clearer:

> "My endpoints have already been getting clear with most of my patients. The issue is that I basically am switching hats very frequently as a practitioner from some patients where we're basically doing ongoing stress management care or more like long-term life focused support to doing specific courses of care with specific outcomes. So by focusing in the outcomes, I think we're going to be in a better position to refine specific aspects of the medicine and work more easily with other practitioners."
