# Onboarding Call Part 2 — Peter Caron, Gotham Acupuncture: Growth Strategy

Continuation of the discovery call. This section covers the doctor referral pipeline in depth, authority positioning, the hiring/training model, revenue targets, expansion timeline, and the competitive moat.

## Doctor Referral Pipeline — Letting Doctors Sell For You

Peter's core insight: doctors already have authority and patient relationships. Rather than doing a hard sell directly to vulnerable patients, get their doctors to prescribe the care.

On post-stroke patients specifically:

> "If I'm trying to pitch that person off the street that feels like a huge ask. I'm talking about something like a thousand dollars a week for them to come in. If their doctor is telling them to come in for that, then they're much more likely to do that. And so this is part of the B2B doctor to doctor approach to doing this, is that I can get someone else to sell my services for me, someone who already has authority. Who already has a relationship with the patients."

On the ethical dimension of not hard-selling vulnerable patients:

> "I'm very aware that while acupuncture has become much more accepted than it used to be, there are still plenty of people who are like, I don't know if I'm gonna commit to like $4,000 a month for care from you while I'm in this vulnerable post stroke state. And I can look at it and go, this is gonna be worth it for you. But I also don't want to do a hard sell when you're in this vulnerable state. So what I want is for your doctor to say, you need to go see these guys for the next three months."

Same pattern applies to digestive issues:

> "Western medicine once again has a very limited tool set here. And so these patients, if their doctors are saying, look, you've been having severe bloating and abdominal pain for a few months. We've done scans, we know there's nothing major wrong with you. These are the people we recommend for it. Then we've got once again really good buy in."

## The Doctor Vetting Problem — An Underserved Need

Peter identified a key insight about why doctors don't refer to acupuncturists more:

> "I can't tell you the number of times I've talked to a doctor who's gone, I've always wanted an acupuncturist to refer patients to. But I haven't known how to find one. I don't know how to vet one. I'm convinced that acupuncture is good. I'm not convinced that any individual acupuncturist is good."

Scarlet validated this: "A lot of acupuncturists are just fine, right? And that's very different from can actually treat an acute or very deep chronic condition."

Peter confirmed: "Yeah, exactly."

## Authority Positioning — Meeting Doctors Where They Are

The book Peter and Jordan co-authored serves dual purposes — technical contribution and credentialing:

> "Part of this is just setting ourselves up to be authorities within the field which has both a technical and a marketing aspect to it. If people simply know us that creates a certain level of authority right there. We've published a book, even if no one ever reads it, that does a thing."

On why specialization is essential for doctor relationships:

> "A lot of acupuncturers, because they're super generalists, that actually lowers their authority with doctors. Doctors are like, I'm an oncologist. I'm an orthopedist. And then I'm up there saying like, Oh well, I'm a digestion specialist and pain specialist and musculoskeletal specialist, migraine specialist. You run down this whole list and they kind of look at you and they're like, that's not possible."

The key framing:

> "What we're talking about doing, at least in the way it faces outwards, it's much less true to Chinese medicine but it's much more true to the culture. We're really looking to meet doctors where they are as opposed to asking them to meet us where we are."

Scarlet crystallized this: "An oncologist, it's like, oh cool, I understand specialty. I trained for specialty. And now you're nerds together. Instead of you being a holistic practitioner."

## Niche Expansion Strategy — Land and Expand

Peter described a deliberate sequencing of how specialties are introduced to referral partners:

> "This doesn't mean we wouldn't treat other things. And so I can say, on the side, if I've built a good relationship with a doctor and they've been sending people to me for low back pain, I can mention like, you know, I'm really good with migraines too. But that's a later sell."

The four core niches (post-stroke, pelvic floor, digestion, anxiety/depression) are the entry point. Additional specialties like migraines are "later sells" once trust is established.

Peter noted they're still finalizing the niche list: "We're still batting around exactly what we're trying to hone in on for specialties because we think that's about all we can get away with before you start to dilute the brand."

## Hiring and Training Model — Building Specialist Practitioners

The plan is to hire vetted recent graduates and train them as specialists:

> "If we're training up relatively recent graduates, like vetted recent graduates that we really trust, we're gonna be sending them to classes to up their skills. We're gonna be training them ourselves. We are going to have to actually make them specialists. Because they're not even generalists yet. So we're gonna have to train them up in specific specialties."

Retention strategy — golden handcuffs:

> "The basic thing we're gonna have to do there is put some golden handcuffs on them. We're gonna have to pay them quite well. And they're gonna have to get to the point where it's like, they could duplicate our protocols. They can't duplicate our business."

## The Competitive Moat — Time, Not IP

Scarlet asked about the moat. Peter's honest assessment:

> "It's a good question. It's multiple factors — logistics, relationships. I mean, it's mostly logistics and relationships. Anything we're doing, someone else can repeat if they've got the skill set to do it."

> "I think the moat is actually more time than anything else. Like, you can duplicate this. It's gonna take you five years."

Retention as part of the moat:

> "This is part of why we want to pay them enough money and have opportunities for advancement in the future. Like, hey, if we're starting another one, we're gonna need to bump you up to a different role within this organization. Now you're going to be one of the primary doctors working at this site."

## Expansion Path — Franchise-Like Growth

Scarlet identified a natural expansion path from the niche model:

> "Inherent in that business model, you have a natural expansion path personally because you could start another location and treat the other specialty there. But you could also offer that as a carrot to your apprentice, where you say you're gonna come up with these specialties but in the future, these are ours, but everything else minus these other ones we've already scoped out, you could do in the future and that wouldn't compete with us. In fact, we'll do it with our blessing and franchise you or something."

## Revenue and Timeline

Peter shared specific financial targets:

> "The goal is — our initial math is that if we're at 70% occupancy, there should be about a $3.5 million gross business."

Timeline for proving the model:

> "If that's running well within say 18 months of starting, and Jordan has a strong marketing background, I'm fairly confident."

Expansion timeline: approximately 2 years to start opening additional locations.

> "I think we're looking at like two years down the line to start opening more locations like this. And then moving these acupuncturists we've hired up."

## AI Use — Content Generation and Consistency

When Scarlet asked about how Peter segments and communicates the long-tail detail about services to referral partners, Peter identified this as a key AI use case:

> "Frankly, some aspects of that are where we're hoping to be able to use AI more and more — to generate those sorts of things in a consistent and reliable way, or in a way where an assistant can make sure it's consistent and reliable."
